. RUWANMALI MAHA SAYA

The Ruwanwelisaya, also known as the Maha Thupa, is a magnificent Buddhist stupa located in the sacred city of Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka. Built by the heroic King Dutugemunu in 140 BCE, it stands as a marvel of ancient engineering, rising to a height of about 338 feet. It is deeply revered worldwide because it enshrines the largest collection of the Buddha's relics. Recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site and a key part of the Atamasthana, its unique bubble-shaped structure is surrounded by a famous wall adorned with hundreds of carved elephants.==

JETHAVANARAMAYA

The Jetavanaramaya is a monumental Buddhist stupa in Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka. Built by King Mahasen in the 3rd Century CE, it represents a pinnacle of ancient engineering as the largest and tallest brick structure in the ancient world, originally standing at about 400 feet. It is deeply revered by Buddhists because it is believed to enshrine a part of the Buddha’s sacred waist girdle (Bandhana Kaaya Bandhana). As a UNESCO World Heritage site, this massive stupa signifies the great power of the Jetavana monastic sect and remains a breathtaking symbol of Sri Lankan heritage.

SHRI MAHA BODHIYA

The Jaya Sri Maha Bodhi is a sacred fig tree located in the Mahamewna Gardens of Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka. Brought to the island in 249 BCE by Sangamitta Theri, the daughter of Emperor Asoka, it is a sapling from the historical Bodhi tree under which Lord Buddha attained enlightenment. It is celebrated as the oldest living human-planted tree in the world with a known plantation date. Today, this deeply revered living monument serves as a central pillar of Buddhist faith, drawing millions of pilgrims worldwide for blessings and meditation.

THUPARAMAYA

The Thuparamaya is the very first Buddhist stupa built in Sri Lanka after the introduction of Buddhism to the island. Located in the sacred city of Anuradhapura, it was constructed by King Devanampiya Tissa in the 3rd Century BCE. This historically significant monument is deeply revered because it enshrines the sacred right collarbone (Aaku Dhatu) of Lord Buddha. Originally built in the shape of a paddy heap, it is famous for its surrounding stone pillars (Vatadage), which once supported a wooden roof, making it a unique architectural gem of Sri Lankan heritage.
LANKARAMAYA
The Lankarama is a beautiful Buddhist stupa located in the sacred city of Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka. Built by King Vattagamini Abhaya (King Walagamba) in the 1st Century BCE, it was anciently known as Silasobbhakandaka Cetiya. The stupa is unique because it was originally built as a Vatadage, a circular relic house with beautifully carved stone pillars that once supported a wooden roof to protect the shrine. Today, it remains a tranquil and highly revered place of worship for pilgrims.
ABHAYAGIRIYA
The Abhayagiriya is a major Buddhist monastic complex and a magnificent stupa located in Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka. Built by King Vattagamini Abhaya (King Walagamba) in the 1st Century BCE, it historically grew into a massive international center for Buddhist studies, attracting scholars from across Asia like China's Fa-Hsien. The stupa itself is one of the tallest brick structures in the world. It is highly famous for its breathtaking Buddhist art, including the Samadhi Buddha Statue, beautiful guardstones, and the Moonstone (Sandakada Pahana). MIRISAWATIYA The Mirisavetiya is a historically important Buddhist stupa located in the sacred city of Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka. Built by King Dutugemunu in the 2nd Century BCE, it was the very first stupa he constructed after defeating King Elara and unifying the country. According to legend, the king built it on the exact spot where his ceremonial scepter (Jayakontha), which contained a sacred relic of the Buddha, became firmly fixed in the ground and could not be moved. It stands as a beautiful symbol of victory and deep faith. LOWAMAHAPAYA The Lovamahaprasadaya (also known as the Brazen Palace) is a unique historical structure located between the Jaya Sri Maha Bodhi and Ruwanwelisaya in Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka. Originally built by King Dutugemunu in the 2nd Century BCE, it was a massive nine-story monastic building with 1,000 rooms to accommodate thousands of monks. It earned the name "Brazen Palace" because its roof was covered with bronze or copper tiles that gleamed in the sun. Today, only 1,600 stone pillars stand as a silent testament to its ancient grandeur.
